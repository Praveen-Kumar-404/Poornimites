UNIT-1

Q1. Concept of Property and Intellectual Property; Difference

1. Property (General Concept)

Property refers to legally recognized rights over tangible or intangible assets. Core elements:

  • Ownership → control over use
  • Exclusivity → right to exclude others
  • Transferability → can be sold, leased, gifted

Traditional property includes:

  • Land
  • Buildings
  • Physical goods

2. Intellectual Property (IP)

Intellectual Property refers to creations of the human mind, protected by law.

Examples:

  • Inventions (patents)
  • Literary/artistic works (copyright)
  • Brand names (trademarks)

Core nature:

  • Intangible
  • Knowledge-based
  • Legally protected for a limited time

3. Key Differences

Aspect

Traditional Property

Intellectual Property

Nature

Physical

Intangible

Creation

Exists naturally or produced

Created by intellect

Ownership Duration

Unlimited

Time-bound

Usage

One person at a time

Possession-based

Law-based (registration/rights)

Physical transfer

4. Core Insight

Traditional property is about control over scarce physical resources.
IP is about control over replicable ideas.

Q2. Theories of Intellectual Property Rights

1. Labor Theory

Associated with John Locke

  • Idea: A person owns what they create through effort
  • Justification: Labor adds value → ownership deserved

Limitation:
Does not define limits of ownership (how much control is fair)

2. Personality Theory

Associated with G. W. F. Hegel

  • Idea: Creations reflect personality
  • IP protects personal expression

Application:
Strong in copyright (art, literature)

3. Utilitarian / Economic Theory

  • IP exists to maximize overall social welfare
  • Provides incentive to innovate
  • Temporary monopoly → encourages invention

Example Logic:
Without patents, inventions would be copied instantly → no incentive

4. Social Planning Theory

  • IP should promote:
  • Cultural development
  • Knowledge access
  • Social justice

5. Core Insight

No single theory is sufficient. Modern IPR is a hybrid system:

  • Reward creators (Locke)
  • Protect personality (Hegel)
  • Promote innovation (Utilitarian)

Q3. IPR as an Instrument of Development

1. Economic Development

IPR drives:

  • Innovation
  • Industrial growth
  • Foreign investment

Mechanism:

  • Protection → confidence → investment → growth

2. Technological Advancement

Patents require disclosure of invention
→ knowledge becomes public
→ further innovation builds on it

3. Trade and Global Integration

Through TRIPS Agreement:

  • Countries adopt uniform IP standards
  • Facilitates international trade

4. Industry-Specific Impact

  • Pharmaceuticals
    Patents encourage drug development
  • IT & Software
    Copyright protects code
  • Brand Economy
    Trademarks create brand value

5. Example Logic

  • A company invents a drug
  • Patent protection → exclusive sales
  • Profit → reinvestment into R&D
  • New drugs developed

Cycle continues → economic development

6. Criticism / Limitation

  • High prices due to monopoly
  • Access issues (especially in developing countries)
  • Balance required between innovation and public interest

7. Core Insight

IPR is not just legal protection.
It is a policy tool to control the flow of knowledge, innovation, and economic power.

Q4. National and International Perspectives on IPR Protection

1. National Perspective

Each country designs IPR policy based on:

  • Economic stage (developing vs developed)
  • Industrial capacity
  • Public interest (health, education)

Key Objectives

  • Promote domestic innovation
  • Protect local industries
  • Ensure public access (e.g., affordable medicines)

Example (India)

  • The Patents Act, 1970 initially allowed only process patents for drugs
    → ensured low-cost medicines
  • Later amended (2005) to comply with TRIPS Agreement

Core Tensio

  • Protection vs accessibility

2. International Perspective

Driven by globalization and trade.

Key Drivers

  • Uniform protection standards
  • Cross-border enforcement
  • Protection of multinational investments

Major Framework

  • TRIPS Agreement under World Trade Organization

Key Features

  • Minimum standards for IP protection
  • Dispute settlement mechanism
  • Binding on member nations

3. Developed vs Developing Conflict

  • Developed countries → strong IPR (protect innovation)
  • Developing countries → flexible IPR (ensure access)

4. Core Insight

National IPR = policy tool
International IPR = trade obligation

Q5. Types of Intellectual Property (Origin and Development)

1. Patent

  • Protects inventions
  • Origin: Industrial Revolution
  • Development: formalized via Paris Convention

2. Copyright

  • Protects literary and artistic works
  • Origin: Printing press era
  • Focus: author’s rights

3. Trademark

  • Protects brand identity (logos, names)
  • Origin: ancient trade markings
  • Modern role: brand value and consumer trust

4. Industrial Design

  • Protects aesthetic appearance of products
  • Focus: shape, pattern, design

5. Geographical Indications (GI)

  • Protects region-specific products
  • Example logic: product quality tied to location

6. Trade Secrets

  • Protects confidential business information
  • No registration required
  • Protection through secrecy

7. Core Insight

All types serve one purpose:
convert knowledge into legally protected economic assets

Q6. Role of International Institutions in IPR Protection

1. Need for International Institutions

IP is global; infringement crosses borders.
Requires coordination beyond national laws.

2. Key Institutions

World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO)

  • Develops global IP treaties
  • Promotes harmonization
  • Provides international registration systems

World Trade Organization (WTO)

  • Enforces IP through trade law
  • Administers TRIPS Agreement
  • Provides dispute settlement mechanism

3. Functions of International Institutions

  • Standard-setting
  • Dispute resolution
  • Technical assistance to countries
  • Capacity building

4. Enforcement Mechanism

  • WTO can impose trade sanctions
  • Ensures compliance across nations

5. Core Insight

  • WIPO → creates rules
  • WTO → enforces rules

Together they form the global IPR control system

Q7. Structure, Functions, and Objectives of WIPO

World Intellectual Property Organization — Structure

  • General Assembly
    Highest decision-making body; all member states
  • Coordination Committee
    Advisory and executive coordination
  • International Bureau
    Administrative secretariat (day-to-day operations)

Functions

  • Administers international treaties (e.g., patents, trademarks)
  • Provides global filing systems (PCT, Madrid System)
  • Promotes harmonization of IP laws
  • Offers technical assistance to developing countries
  • Maintains IP databases and information systems

Objectives

  • Promote protection of IP worldwide
  • Encourage innovation and creativity
  • Facilitate technology transfer
  • Balance rights of creators and public interest

Core Insight

WIPO is a norm-setting and facilitation body, not an enforcement authority.

Q9. Dispute Settlement Mechanism in IPR (International Level)

1. Need

IP disputes are cross-border:

  • Patent infringement across countries
  • Trademark misuse in global markets

2. WTO Dispute Settlement (Primary System)

Under World Trade Organization and TRIPS:

Process

  1. Consultation between countries
  2. Panel formation
  3. Panel report
  4. Appeal (Appellate Body)
  5. Implementation of ruling

Enforcement

  • Trade sanctions for non-compliance

3. Role of WIPO

  • Provides mediation and arbitration
  • Handles domain name disputes (UDRP)
  • Offers alternative dispute resolution (ADR)

4. New Trends / “New Territories”

  • Digital IP disputes (software, streaming, AI content)
  • Domain name conflicts
  • Cross-border data ownership issues

5. Key Features

  • Rule-based system
  • Binding decisions (WTO)
  • Emphasis on negotiation before litigation

Core Insight

International IPR dispute settlement is state-driven (WTO) with supplementary private resolution (WIPO ADR).

Q10. Commercialization of Intellectual Property through Licensing

1. Concept

Licensing = granting permission to another party to use IP without transferring ownership.

  • Owner (licensor) retains rights
  • User (licensee) pays consideration

2. Purpose

  • Monetization of IP
  • Market expansion without direct investment
  • Technology transfer

3. Types of Licensing

  • Exclusive License
    Only one licensee; even owner cannot use
  • Non-Exclusive License
    Multiple licensees allowed
  • Sole License
    One licensee + owner retains usage
  • Compulsory License
    Government permits use without owner consent (public interest)

4. Key Elements of License Agreement

  • Scope (territory, duration)
  • Rights granted
  • Payment terms
  • Confidentiality
  • Termination clauses

5. Core Insight

Licensing converts legal rights into recurring revenue streams.

Q14. Concept and Objectives of Patent Law

1. Concept of Patent

Patent = exclusive legal right granted for an invention.

  • Limited period (generally 20 years)
  • Territorial (valid only in granted country)

2. Objectives of Patent Law

  • Encourage innovation
  • Reward inventors
  • Promote disclosure of knowledge
  • Support industrial growth

3. Functional Mechanism

  • Inventor discloses invention publicly
  • In return receives temporary monopoly

4. Balance Principle

  • Incentive to inventor
  • Access to society after expiry

5. Core Insight

Patent law is a trade-off system:
temporary monopoly in exchange for permanent knowledge.

Q15. Provisions and Importance of the Paris Convention

Paris Convention

1. Purpose

First major international agreement for protection of industrial property.

2. Key Provisions

  • National Treatment
    Foreign applicants treated same as domestic
  • Right of Priority
    Applicant can file in multiple countries within a time window
    (12 months for patents)
  • Independence of Patents
    Patent in one country independent of others

3. Importance

  • Simplifies international patent filing
  • Encourages global protection of inventions
  • Reduces duplication of effort

4. Limitations

  • Does not provide a single global patent
  • Only sets principles, not enforcement

5. Core Insight

Paris Convention establishes procedural convenience, not centralized protection.

Q16. Objectives and Working of the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT)

Patent Cooperation Treaty

1. Objective

  • Simplify filing of patents in multiple countries
  • Reduce duplication of effort
  • Provide a unified initial procedure

2. Key Features

  • Single international application
  • Delays need for multiple national filings
  • Provides preliminary examination

3. Working (Process Flow)

Stage 1: International Phase

  • File one application (Receiving Office)
  • International Search Report (ISR) generated
  • Publication of application
  • Optional International Preliminary Examination

Stage 2: National Phase

  • Applicant enters chosen countries
  • Each country examines under its own law
  • Patent granted or rejected individually

4. Advantages

  • Time (up to ~30 months before national entry)
  • Cost efficiency (initial stage)
  • Better decision-making (based on search report)

5. Limitation

No “global patent” is granted.

Core Insight

PCT centralizes procedure, not grant of rights.

Q17. Need for Harmonization between CBD and TRIPS

Relevant Frameworks

  • TRIPS Agreement
  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)

1. Conflict

TRIPS

  • Protects inventions (including biotech)
  • Promotes patent rights

CBD

  • Protects biological resources
  • Ensures sovereignty of nations
  • Requires benefit sharing

2. Key Issues

  • Biopiracy (use of traditional knowledge without consent)
  • Patenting of natural resources
  • Lack of compensation to source communities

3. Need for Harmonization

  • Ensure fair use of biological resources
  • Protect indigenous knowledge
  • Balance innovation with equity

4. Mechanisms

  • Disclosure of origin in patent applications
  • Prior informed consent
  • Access and Benefit Sharing (ABS)

5. Core Insight

TRIPS protects innovation ownership.
CBD protects resource ownership.
Conflict arises when innovation uses natural resources.

The Patents Act, 1970

Q19. Amendments to the Patents Act, 1970 (TRIPS Compliance)

Context

India was required to align its patent law with the TRIPS Agreement under the World Trade Organization.

1. 1999 Amendment

  • Introduced Mailbox System
    → Allowed filing of product patent applications (especially pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals)
  • Provided Exclusive Marketing Rights (EMR)

2. 2002 Amendment

  • Patent term extended to 20 years (uniform standard)
  • Defined inventive step clearly
  • Strengthened examination procedures

3. 2005 Amendment (Most Critical)

  • Introduced Product Patents in pharmaceuticals and chemicals
  • Removed earlier restriction of only process patents
  • Strengthened compulsory licensing provisions
  • Introduced Section 3(d)
    → Prevents evergreening (minor modifications not patentable)

4. Impact

  • Compliance with global standards
  • Increased foreign investment
  • Maintained public health safeguards

Core Insight

Amendments reflect a forced alignment with global trade law, with controlled retention of domestic safeguards.

Q20. Patentable Subject Matter (Indian Law)

Under The Patents Act, 1970

1. What Can Be Patented

An invention must be:

  • New
  • Useful
  • Capable of industrial application

Examples:

  • Machines
  • Chemical compositions
  • Processes

2. What Cannot Be Patented (Key Exclusions)

  • Discoveries (natural substances)
  • Scientific principles
  • Mathematical methods
  • Business methods
  • Computer programs (as such)
  • Traditional knowledge
  • Methods of medical treatment

3. Special Provision

  • Section 3(d): prevents patents on minor modifications of known substances

4. Core Insight

Patent law protects applications of knowledge, not knowledge itself.

UNIT-5

Q21. Criteria for Patentability (India)

1. Novelty

  • Invention must be new
  • Not disclosed anywhere before (global standard)

2. Inventive Step (Non-Obviousness)

  • Must not be obvious to a skilled person in the field
  • Must show technical advancement or economic significance

3. Industrial Applicability

  • Must be capable of being made or used in industry

4. Additional Conditions

  • Must not fall under non-patentable categories
  • Must comply with legal disclosure requirements

5. Core Insight

Patentability is a filter mechanism:
only inventions that are new + non-obvious + useful pass through.

Q22. Procedure for Filing and Grant of Patent in India

Under The Patents Act, 1970

1. Filing Stage

  • Provisional Application (optional)
    → Secures early priority date
  • Complete Specification
    → Full details of invention

Documents include:

  • Description
  • Claims
  • Drawings (if required)

2. Publication

  • Application published after 18 months
  • Early publication possible on request

3. Examination

  • Request for Examination (RFE) must be filed
  • Patent Office examines:
  • Novelty
  • Inventive step
  • Industrial applicability
  • Issues First Examination Report (FER)

4. Response by Applicant

  • Applicant must reply to objections
  • Amendments may be made

5. Grant of Patent

  • If all conditions satisfied → patent granted
  • Published in official journal

6. Opposition

  • Pre-grant opposition (before grant)
  • Post-grant opposition (after grant)

Core Insight

Patent process is a filtering pipeline ensuring only valid inventions receive protection.

Q23. Patent Infringement: Types and Remedies

1. Concept

Infringement = unauthorized use of a patented invention.

2. Types of Infringement

  • Direct Infringement
    Unauthorized making, using, selling
  • Indirect Infringement
    Assisting or inducing infringement

3. Remedies under Indian Law

Under The Patents Act, 1970:

  • Injunction
  • Court order to stop infringement
  • Damages
  • Compensation for loss
  • Account of Profits
  • Infringer must hand over profits
  • Seizure/Destruction
  • Infringing goods removed

4. Defenses

  • Patent invalidity
  • Non-infringement
  • Use for research or education

Core Insight

Infringement law protects exclusive rights by imposing economic consequences.

Q24. Provisions of the Biological Diversity Act, 2002

Biological Diversity Act, 2002

1. Objective

  • Conservation of biodiversity
  • Sustainable use
  • Fair sharing of benefits

2. Key Provisions

  • Regulation of Access
  • Foreign entities require approval to use biological resources
  • National Biodiversity Authority (NBA)
  • Governing body for approvals
  • Protection of Traditional Knowledge
  • Prevents unauthorized use
  • Benefit Sharing
  • Ensures local communities receive compensation

3. Restrictions

  • Prior approval required before applying for patents using biological resources

4. Core Insight

The Act shifts control from individual innovators → national and community ownership of resources.

Q25. Access and Benefit Sharing (ABS) under the Biological Diversity Act, 2002

Under Biological Diversity Act, 2002

1. Concept of ABS

Access and Benefit Sharing ensures that:

  • Those who use biological resources
  • Must share benefits with those who provide or conserve them

2. Why ABS Exists

Prevents:

  • Biopiracy (unauthorized use of biological resources)
  • Exploitation of indigenous knowledge

Ensures:

  • Fair compensation
  • Ethical use of biodiversity

3. Key Components

Access

  • Prior approval required from National Biodiversity Authority (NBA)
  • Applies especially to:
  • Foreign individuals/companies
  • Use for research, commercial purposes, patents

Benefit Sharing

Benefits may include:

  • Monetary (royalties, fees)
  • Non-monetary (technology transfer, training, joint research)

4. Mechanism

  1. User applies for access
  2. Authority evaluates purpose
  3. Terms of benefit sharing decided
  4. Agreement enforced

5. Stakeholders

  • Local communities
  • Government authorities
  • Companies/researchers

6. Link with Patents

  • Patent applications involving biological resources require disclosure
  • Benefit sharing must be ensured before commercialization

7. Core Insight

ABS transforms biodiversity from a free resource → regulated economic asset with shared ownership rights.
