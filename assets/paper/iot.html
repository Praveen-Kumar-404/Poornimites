Internet of Things

UNIT 1

1. Characteristics and Functional Blocks of IoT


Characteristics:
• Connectivity enables devices to communicate across heterogeneous networks.
• Intelligence arises from embedded processing and analytics.
• Dynamic changes allow devices to adapt to context, such as mobility or environmental changes.
• Scalability supports large deployments of sensors and nodes.
• Heterogeneity allows integration of different hardware, software, and communication technologies.
• Security ensures confidentiality, integrity, and authentication.
• Data-driven operation uses collected data to trigger actions and decisions.

Functional Blocks:
• Device: sensors and actuators that interact with the physical world.
• Communication: wired or wireless interfaces that transport data.
• Services: identity, authentication, data processing, and analytics.
• Application: domain-specific logic such as home automation or industrial control.
• Management: configuration, monitoring, update, diagnostics.
• Security: encryption, access control, trust management.

2. Physical and Logical Design of IoT


 Physical design:
Focuses on real components.
• IoT devices: contain microcontrollers, sensors, actuators.
• Sensors: capture physical parameters (temperature, motion).
• Actuators: perform actions (switching, movement).
• Communication modules: Wi-Fi, BLE, ZigBee, NFC, cellular.
• Power units: batteries, energy harvesting.
• Edge nodes and gateways: connect local networks to the internet.

Logical design:
Describes structure, behavior, and abstract models.
• Functional blocks define how data flows through sensing, processing, communication, and application layers.
• Communication models (request–response, publish–subscribe) define data exchange patterns.
• IoT protocols (MQTT, CoAP, HTTP) define messaging rules.
• Logical components: data ingestion, filtering, storage, analytics, visualization.
• Abstraction enables system-level understanding without hardware detail.

3. Comparison of IoT Communication Models


 Request–Response:
• Client sends a request; server returns response.
• Synchronous, direct interaction.
• Used when immediate data is needed, such as polling a sensor.
• Drawback: increased traffic due to repeated requests.

Publish–Subscribe:
 • Publisher sends data to a broker; subscribers receive relevant messages.
• Asynchronous, decoupled interaction.
• Supports large-scale IoT systems with distributed nodes.
• Efficient for real-time updates (MQTT uses this model).

Push–Pull:
 • Data producers push information into a queue or buffer.
• Consumers pull data when required.
• Provides load balancing and decouples production from consumption rate.
• Suitable for data streams where processing may not be real time.

4. IoT Communication Protocols


 Purpose: define how data is formatted, transmitted, routed, and received between IoT devices and cloud or gateway systems. Protocols are selected based on power limits, bandwidth, reliability, and application domain.

Categories:
• Network layer protocols:
– IPv6: large address space for billions of IoT devices.
– 6LoWPAN: adapts IPv6 for low-power wireless networks through header compression.
– RPL: routing for constrained networks using Directed Acyclic Graphs.

• Transport and messaging protocols:
– MQTT: lightweight publish–subscribe for low-power devices; uses a broker.
– CoAP: lightweight REST-like protocol over UDP; supports constrained devices.
– AMQP: reliable message queuing for enterprise IoT.
– HTTP/HTTPS: widely used but heavy for constrained nodes.

• Communication technologies:
– Bluetooth/BLE for short range.
– ZigBee for mesh networking.
– NFC for very short range.
– Wi-Fi for high bandwidth.
– Cellular (4G/5G) for long-range, high mobility.

UNIT 2

1. Sensors and Actuators


 Sensors: input devices that detect physical or environmental conditions and convert them into electrical signals.
Types:
• Temperature (thermistors, RTDs).
• Motion (PIR).
• Proximity (IR, ultrasonic).
• Humidity (capacitive humidity sensors).
• Gas (MQ-series).
• Pressure (piezoresistive sensors).
• Light (LDR, photodiodes).

Working principle: physical change → transducer → analog/digital signal → microcontroller.

Actuators: output devices that perform physical action in response to electrical input.
Types:
• Motors (DC, servo, stepper).
• Relays (electrically controlled switches).
• Solenoids (linear motion).
• Valves (fluid control).

Working principle: electrical signal → mechanical motion, switching, or controlling.
Role in IoT: sensors gather data; actuators execute decisions such as turning ON a motor or locking a door.

2. Wireless Sensor Network (WSN) Architecture
WSN is a distributed network of sensor nodes communicating wirelessly to monitor physical conditions.

Node architecture:
 • Sensor unit: measures environmental parameters.
• Microcontroller: processes sensed data.
• Radio transceiver: handles wireless communication (IEEE 802.15.4, ZigBee, BLE).
• Power unit: battery or energy-harvesting module.
• Optional: memory, location unit (GPS), actuator interface.

Network architecture:
• Sensor nodes form clusters or mesh networks.
• Cluster-head nodes aggregate and forward data.
• Multi-hop routing delivers data from nodes to base station or gateway.
• Gateway connects the WSN to the internet/cloud.

Design goals: low power usage, scalability, robustness to node failure, self-organization, efficient routing.

3. How Nodes Communicate in a Wireless Sensor Network.


Communication operates through short-range radios using low-power protocols such as IEEE 802.15.4, ZigBee, or BLE.
Core mechanisms:
• Broadcasting: nodes announce availability or status to nearby nodes.
• Multi-hop routing: data is forwarded through intermediate nodes instead of a single long-range transmission.
• Cluster-based communication: cluster-head nodes collect data from member nodes, compress or aggregate it, and forward it to the base station.
• TDMA/CSMA access: nodes share the wireless medium using scheduled time slots (TDMA) or collision-avoidance carrier sensing (CSMA).
• Sleep–wake cycles: radios stay off most of the time, waking only to sense or transmit, minimizing power consumption.
Data path: sensor node → neighboring node(s) → cluster-head or router → gateway/base station → cloud or server.
Communication emphasizes low power, reliability in noisy environments, and self-healing routing when nodes fail.


4. Node Deployment and Connectivity


Deployment defines how sensor nodes are placed in the environment; connectivity defines the ability of nodes to form a functioning network.

Deployment models:
• Random deployment: nodes are scattered in large or inaccessible areas (forests, disaster zones). Topology self-organizes automatically.
• Grid deployment: nodes placed at fixed intervals for uniform coverage.
• Cluster deployment: nodes grouped around cluster-heads to reduce transmission distance.
• Manual placement: specific locations selected for targeted monitoring.

Connectivity principles:
• Nodes must remain within radio range of at least one neighbor to maintain the network.
• Redundant nodes create fault-tolerance; if a node dies, alternative routes maintain connectivity.
• Network density affects routing: sparse networks risk disconnections; dense networks improve reliability but increase collisions.
• Energy-aware connectivity: nodes adjust transmission power to preserve battery life while maintaining communication links.
Connectivity performance depends on topology, radio range, deployment area size, and environmental obstacles.

5. Compare Arduino and Raspberry Pi Development Boards


 Arduino:
• Microcontroller-based (ATmega series).
• Runs a single embedded program; no operating system.
• Real-time control of sensors and actuators due to deterministic timing.
• Interfaces: GPIO, PWM, ADC, I2C, SPI, UART.
• Suitable for simple, low-power, hardware-level tasks (temperature monitoring, motor control).
• Programmed through Arduino IDE with C/C++ style sketches.

Raspberry Pi:
• Microprocessor-based; full Linux operating system.
• Capable of multitasking, running servers, databases, and high-level applications.
• Interfaces: GPIO, USB, HDMI, Ethernet, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth.
• Supports Python, C, Java, Node.js, and full OS services.
• Suitable for applications requiring computation, networking, multimedia, or cloud connectivity (edge computing, camera processing).

Comparison:
• Performance: Raspberry Pi is significantly more powerful; Arduino is minimal and deterministic.
• Power consumption: Arduino is low power; Raspberry Pi is high power.
• Use-case: Arduino for direct sensor/actuator control; Raspberry Pi for analytics, communication, and complex applications.
• Complexity: Arduino is simpler; Raspberry Pi requires OS-level configuration.

UNIT 3

1. Compare ZigBee, BLE, NFC, and Z-Wave.


 ZigBee:
• Based on IEEE 802.15.4.
• Mesh networking with large node counts.
• Low power, moderate range (10–100 m).
• Used in home automation, industrial monitoring.

BLE (Bluetooth Low Energy):
• Point-to-point, broadcast, and mesh support.
• Very low power; short–medium range (~10–50 m).
• Optimized for wearables, health sensors, mobile-connected IoT.

NFC (Near Field Communication):
• Extremely short range (≤4 cm).
• No pairing necessary; tap-based communication.
• Used for payments, access control, identification.
• Low data rate; not suitable for sensor networks.

Z-Wave:
• Proprietary low-power mesh technology.
• Operates in sub-GHz bands → less interference than 2.4 GHz devices.
• Range ~30–100 m.
• Focused on smart home automation (lights, locks, sensors).

Comparison summary:
• Range: NFC < BLE < ZigBee/Z-Wave.
• Network type: ZigBee/Z-Wave = mesh; BLE optional mesh; NFC none.
• Power consumption: NFC lowest (passive), BLE low, ZigBee/Z-Wave moderate.
• Use-cases: NFC for authentication; BLE for personal devices; ZigBee/Z-Wave for home/industrial IoT.

2. WPAN (IEEE 802.15.4) Architecture with Diagram Description


 IEEE 802.15.4 defines the physical and MAC layers for low-rate, low-power wireless networks. It is the foundation for ZigBee, 6LoWPAN, Thread, and many WSN systems.

Physical Layer (PHY):
• Modulation (O-QPSK, BPSK) and frequency bands (2.4 GHz, 915 MHz, 868 MHz).
• Handles transmission, reception, energy detection, clear-channel assessment, link quality.

MAC Layer:
• Manages channel access using CSMA/CA.
• Frame formats, addressing (16-bit short or 64-bit extended addresses).
• Beacon and non-beacon modes for synchronization.
• Guaranteed Time Slots (GTS) for time-critical traffic.

Network Structure:
• Two device types:
– FFD (Full-Function Device): can act as coordinator or router.
– RFD (Reduced-Function Device): simple end nodes.
• Topologies supported: star, peer-to-peer, mesh (via higher protocols like ZigBee).

Diagram description:
Application Layer ← not part of 802.15.4
Network Layer ← not part of 802.15.4
MAC Layer ← medium access, addressing, framing
PHY Layer ← modulation, signaling, RF transmission

4. Remote Monitoring and Sensing:


 Remote monitoring: observing and tracking environmental or system parameters from a distant location through networked sensors.
Core components:
• Sensors collect real-world data: temperature, motion, gas, vibration.
• IoT nodes preprocess the data.
• Wireless transmission through ZigBee, BLE, Wi-Fi, cellular, or 6LoWPAN.
• Cloud/storage receives continuous data streams.
• Dashboards or alerts represent the monitored values.

Functions:
 • Real-time observation without physical presence.
• Automated alerts when values exceed thresholds.
• Historical data storage for trend analysis.

Applications:
 • Industrial equipment monitoring (vibration, fault detection).
• Smart agriculture (soil moisture, humidity).
• Smart cities (air pollution, traffic density).
• Healthcare (remote patient vitals).

Sensing:
• Data acquisition from the environment using transducers.
• Conversion of analog signals to digital for processing.
• Continuous or periodic sampling based on application needs.

3. 6LoWPAN Architecture


6LoWPAN enables IPv6 packets to run over low-power wireless networks built on IEEE 802.15.4. It provides an adaptation layer between the IPv6 network layer and the 802.15.4 MAC/PHY layers.

Purpose:
 • Compress IPv6 headers to fit within the small 127-byte 802.15.4 frame.
• Enable routing, fragmentation, and reassembly for constrained nodes.
• Allow end-to-end IP communication directly from sensor nodes to the internet.

Architecture Layers:
 • IPv6 Layer: standard IPv6 addressing, routing, and datagram structure.
• 6LoWPAN Adaptation Layer:
– Header Compression (HC): compress IPv6, UDP, ICMP headers using context information.
– Fragmentation: splits large IPv6 packets into smaller 802.15.4 frames; reassembly at the destination.
– Mesh Addressing: forwards packets across multi-hop wireless networks.
– Dispatch Format: identifies packet types within the LoWPAN frame.
• IEEE 802.15.4 MAC/PHY:
– Handles radio transmission, CSMA/CA access, energy detection, and radio-level framing.

Key Features:
 • Low power operation suitable for battery-based sensors.
• IPv6 compatibility allows direct integration with the internet.
• Supports mesh networking through route-over or mesh-under models.
• Efficient bandwidth use through compression and optimized routing.

Applications: smart meters, large sensor deployments, building automation.

UNIT 4

1. NodeMCU Board Architecture and Pin Description


 NodeMCU uses the ESP8266 Wi-Fi SoC, integrating Wi-Fi connectivity with GPIO capabilities for IoT prototyping.

Architecture Components:
 • ESP8266EX Microcontroller: 32-bit Tensilica CPU, integrated Wi-Fi module, clock up to 160 MHz.
• Memory: on-chip SRAM, external flash for firmware.
• Wi-Fi Module: 2.4 GHz, supports 802.11 b/g/n, station and soft-AP modes.
• USB–Serial Converter (CH340/CP2102): used for programming from a PC.
• Voltage Regulation: 5 V USB input regulated down to 3.3 V for ESP8266.
• GPIO subsystem: usable pins exposed for user applications.
• ADC: one analog input channel (0–1 V input range).

Pin Description:
 • GPIO Pins (D0–D8): digital I/O for sensors, actuators, communication modules.
– GPIO0, GPIO2, GPIO15 affect boot mode and require proper logic states.
• ADC0: analog input for reading analog sensors.
• UART (RX, TX): programming and serial communication.
• I2C (SDA, SCL): software I2C on GPIO4 and GPIO5.
• SPI (MISO, MOSI, CLK, CS): communication with high-speed peripherals.
• Power pins:
– VIN: 5 V input from USB or external source.
– 3V3: regulated output for low-power modules.
– GND: ground reference.
• RST: reset the board.
• EN (CH_PD): enable pin for powering the chip.

Operational features: deep-sleep support, Wi-Fi stack, lightweight hardware ideal for cloud-connected applications.

2. MQTT Protocol to Build a Publish–Subscribe IoT Application


MQTT is a lightweight messaging protocol designed for constrained devices and unreliable networks. It follows publish–subscribe architecture through a central broker.

Core Components:
 • Publisher: device or service sending messages to a topic.
• Subscriber: device that requests messages from a specific topic.
• Broker: server responsible for receiving all messages and routing them to appropriate subscribers.

Protocol Workflow:
 • Devices connect to broker using TCP (with optional TLS encryption).
• Publisher sends data tagged with a topic (example: home/room1/temp).
• Broker forwards data to all subscribers of that topic.
• Subscribers receive data without needing direct connection to the publisher.

Features:
 • Very low network overhead; ideal for low-power sensors.
• Supports QoS levels:
– QoS 0: at most once.
– QoS 1: at least once.
– QoS 2: exactly once.
• Supports persistent sessions and retained messages.
• Runs efficiently on NodeMCU, Arduino, Raspberry Pi.

Use Cases: environment monitoring, smart home data exchange, machine health diagnostics.

Application Example:
 NodeMCU reads temperature → publishes to topic "/sensor/temp" → cloud MQTT broker → dashboard subscribes → displays real-time values.

1. Big Data


Big Data refers to extremely large, diverse, and continuously growing datasets generated from sensors, devices, social media, transactions, logs, and IoT systems. Traditional data-processing tools cannot efficiently store, analyze, or extract insights from this scale of data.

Types of Data:
• Structured Data: organized into tables or fixed formats (SQL databases, spreadsheets).
• Semi-Structured Data: partially organized using tags or markers (JSON, XML, logs).
• Unstructured Data: no predefined structure (images, audio, video, free text).

Characteristics (5Vs):
• Volume: massive quantities of data from millions of devices.
• Velocity: rapid data generation and arrival speed, often requiring real-time processing.
• Variety: multiple formats such as sensor readings, text, images, audio.
• Veracity: data reliability and accuracy affected by noise, missing values, or errors.
• Value: meaningful insights extracted from data for decisions and automation.

Big Data in IoT:
Sensor networks produce continuous streams of data requiring scalable storage, distributed processing, and analytics engines using Hadoop, Spark, NoSQL databases, and cloud services.

Cloud Computing Platforms for IoT:
Cloud platforms provide essential infrastructure, services, and tools for storing, processing, and managing IoT-generated data.

Core Functions:
• Device Management: provisioning, authentication, monitoring, remote updates.
• Data Storage: scalable databases for time-series, logs, media, and structured data.
• Data Processing: stream processing, batch analytics, machine learning.
• APIs and Integration: REST or MQTT endpoints connecting devices to cloud applications.
• Visualization: dashboards and alerts for real-time monitoring.

Key Cloud Platforms:
• AWS IoT Core: device registry, MQTT broker, Lambda processing, S3 storage.
• Microsoft Azure IoT Hub: device twin, analytics, cloud storage.
• Google Cloud IoT Core (legacy): secure device gateway, Pub/Sub messaging.
• IBM Watson IoT: device management and analytics.
• Open-source platforms: ThingsBoard, Kaa IoT, Node-RED.

Benefits: scalability, reliability, global accessibility, reduced infrastructure cost, built-in analytics, easy integration with machine learning and big data services.

4. Design an IoT-Based Smart Parking System Using Sensors, NodeMCU, and Cloud Storage.


Goal: detect vehicle presence in parking slots, update availability in real time, and store data on the cloud for monitoring.

System Components:
• Sensor Layer: ultrasonic sensors or IR sensors at each parking slot to detect vehicle occupancy.
• Controller Layer: NodeMCU (ESP8266) collects sensor data and provides Wi-Fi connectivity.
• Cloud Layer: cloud database (Firebase, AWS, ThingsBoard) stores parking slot status.
 • Application Layer: dashboard or mobile app displays available/occupied slots.

Architecture:
• Each slot has a sensor → outputs distance or presence signal.
• NodeMCU reads sensor values through GPIO pins.
• NodeMCU processes these values and determines slot state (occupied/free).
• NodeMCU connects to cloud via Wi-Fi and updates the status through REST API or MQTT topic.
• Cloud stores each slot’s state in a real-time database.
• User interface retrieves cloud data and displays parking availability in real time.

Workflow:
• Vehicle enters a slot → sensor detects reduced distance → NodeMCU sends “occupied”.
• Vehicle leaves → sensor reports open status → NodeMCU updates “free”.
• Cloud dashboard refreshes automatically, showing slot counts and location map.

Key Features:
• Real-time monitoring with minimal latency.
• Remote accessibility through mobile or web application.
• Scalable: more slots and NodeMCU units can be added.
• Low-cost components suitable for IoT automation.

Applications: malls, offices, smart cities, residential parking systems.

UNIT 5

1. Legal Challenges and Ethical Issues in IoT.


Legal Challenges:
• Data Privacy: IoT devices continuously collect sensitive data (location, habits, health metrics). Laws such as GDPR require explicit consent, data minimization, and secure handling. Violations arise when devices collect more data than necessary or share data without authorization.
• Data Ownership: ambiguity about who owns the data generated by IoT devices – the user, manufacturer, or service provider. Ownership determines rights to access, modify, or delete data.
• Security and Liability: insecure IoT devices can be hacked, leading to unauthorized access, surveillance, or physical harm. Determining liability for failures (device maker, software vendor, user) is legally complex.
• Cross-Border Data Transfer: cloud platforms often store data in different countries, which may violate local data protection laws.
• Compliance Requirements: industries such as healthcare, automotive, and energy have strict regulations. IoT systems must comply with safety, encryption, and auditing standards.

Ethical Issues:
• Surveillance and Tracking: continuous monitoring of users’ behavior may infringe on personal autonomy and freedom.
• Lack of Transparency: many IoT devices do not clearly inform users about what data is collected and how it's used.
• Bias in Automated Decisions: IoT analytics and AI may create unfair or discriminatory outcomes if data is incomplete or biased.
• Consent and Control: ethical design requires users to have meaningful control over devices, including data access, deletion, and disabling tracking features.
• Environmental Impact: production and disposal of billions of IoT devices contribute to e-waste and carbon footprint.

Ethical IoT design emphasizes transparency, data minimization, security by design, and fair use of collected information.

2. Role of IoT in Agriculture and Environmental Protection.


 Agriculture:
• Precision Farming: IoT sensors measure soil moisture, humidity, temperature, nutrient levels. Data helps optimize irrigation, fertilizer usage, and pesticide application.
• Smart Irrigation Systems: soil moisture sensors and weather data automatically control irrigation pumps, reducing water waste.
• Livestock Monitoring: wearable IoT devices track animal health, movement, and feeding patterns, enabling early detection of diseases.
• Crop Health Monitoring: drones and imaging sensors identify pest attacks and nutrient deficiencies early.
• Supply Chain Tracking: RFID and GPS-based IoT devices track agricultural produce from field to market, improving transparency and reducing spoilage.

Benefits: higher crop yield, reduced water and fertilizer usage, improved resource efficiency, lower operational costs.

Environmental Protection:
• Air Quality Monitoring: IoT sensors measure pollutants (CO₂, PM2.5, NO₂) in real time, enabling rapid response to hazardous conditions.
• Water Quality Monitoring: sensors track pH, turbidity, dissolved oxygen, and contaminants in rivers, lakes, and reservoirs.
• Wildlife Tracking: GPS-enabled IoT tags monitor endangered species’ movement to prevent poaching and support conservation.
• Waste Management: smart bins with fill-level sensors optimize waste collection routes, reducing fuel consumption.
• Disaster Management: distributed sensor networks detect floods, landslides, forest fires, and earthquakes, enabling early warnings.

Impact: improved environmental sustainability, faster detection of ecological threats, and data-driven conservation policies.
